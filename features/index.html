Problem Statement

ENVIRONMENTAL CONSEQUENCES

With as many as 3-10 bark beetle generations per year, outbreaks have caused severe economic losses to federal, state, and private forestry services. While other environmental and sociological consequences are still largely unexplored, deforestation accounts for an estimated $2 billion in expenses per year.

Implications

The source of loss is two-fold: on the one hand detection and containment surveys are expensive, and on the other, the blue-stain fungi secreted by bark beetles devalues the commercial worth of sapwood, adversely affecting the revenue of lumber companies.

10-40 years post-outbreak, moisture content in tree bark is also severely depleted, amplifying the probability of high-severity forest fires.


Inaccurate Sampling .

The Canadian Forestry Service estimates the accuracy of aerial detection flights for classifying the stage of a MPB infestation as ranging between a mere 60-80%. Much of the inaccuracy in these procedures can be traced to time delays associated with manual sampling, in addition to the presence of subtle visual cues insensible to the human eye.

Accessibility Barriers.

Ground reconnaissance surveys are estimated to incur high costs of roughly $230 per acre, according to the U.S. Forestry Service. While operational efficiencies vary between stations, a detailed analysis typically ranges between 2 to 3 hours in time per acre, representing an expense that many underfunded forestry stations cannot afford.

Human Resources.

In relation to forest health management, and more specifically the ground-based evaluation of beetle infestations, common practices entail the expertise of two trained park rangers who follow survey specifications provided by their forestry service. All sampling is conducted manually, requiring the intuition of experienced park rangers.
